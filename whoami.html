asupasuyo on the wire

i've spent most of my life trying to figure out how things work. computers just happened to be the easiest place to start.

unlike physical infra or anything proprietary, software does not hide. you can crack open the linux kernel, read it, and know exactly why it does what it does. no locked doors. for someone with too much curiosity and not much patience, that was everything.

as a kid i broke things constantly. hacked games, rooted phones, flashed custom roms on every android i could get my hands on. once i got my own pc, that curiosity turned into wiping the os, messing with firmware, getting hacked, and eventually hacking my own school's systems. just because pushing every button is the fastest way to map a system, i did without worrying much about the consequences. that loop, completely lost then slowly less lost, is basically my whole personality.

i build things when reality annoys me. campus firewall blocked me, so i wrote a dpi-resistant vpn. screen sharing lagged too much, so i built my own webrtc setup. focusing on lectures was getting difficult so i made my own youtube client. code's never felt like a career to me, more like a crowbar for bending my environment into shape.

but computers were never really the point. same itch shows up in geopolitics, history, philosophy, chess, even myself. all the same thing really - take it apart, see how it ticks, see if you can do it better.

right now i'm deep in low level programming, trying to build os understanding, some cp on side and a mechanical engineering degree from nsut - fulltime on paper, not so much in practice. someday i want aerospace to be next on that list. still pretty distant from here.
